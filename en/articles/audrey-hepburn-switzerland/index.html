The Opposite of Fame Was a Mountain — Why Audrey Hepburn Spent Her Last Years in a Small Swiss Village

Audrey Hepburn spent her final thirty years in Tolochenaz, a small village in Vaud, Switzerland. Why did the UNICEF ambassador keep returning to a village

"The Peaceful Place." That is what she named her house. To name something is to admit you never had it before.

Tolochenaz, canton of Vaud, Switzerland. In a village of vineyards above Lake Geneva stands an eighteenth-century stone farmhouse. She called it La Paisible. The most famous face in the world spent her last thirty years not in Hollywood or Rome, but here.

Across the water rise the mountains of Savoy; on clear mornings, they say, you could make out the white crown of Mont Blanc. What she checked each morning when she opened the curtains was probably not the weather — it was that the mountains were still there.

A Small Chapel on a Mountain

The story begins in September 1954, on a ridge eleven hundred metres above Lake Lucerne. In the little chapel of Bürgenstock, twenty-five-year-old Audrey Hepburn was married for the first time — one year after Roman Holiday made her name known to the world. From then on, whenever the cameras stopped, the place she returned to was not Los Angeles. It was a country of lakes and mountains. She would call Switzerland home for the rest of her life.

The Girl Who Knew Hunger

Born in Brussels in 1929, she spent the Second World War in the occupied Netherlands and survived the Hunger Winter of 1944–45 as a teenager. After the war, international aid kept her alive. "I was one of the children who received help after the war," she would say, again and again. Those who have known hunger understand what a garden full of fruit means. Those who have known bombardment understand that silence is a luxury.
